From 3 Days to 2 Hours: AI-Powered KYC for Tier-1 Banks

Banks were losing customers at the identity verification step. The problem wasn't speed ‚Äî it was silent failure. I found it, fixed it, and grew revenue 25% in 9 months.

‚Üì 40% acquisition time ‚Üë 35% KYC accuracy ‚Üë 25% revenue 3 contract renewals

The Problem

Signzy's KYC platform served Yes Bank and IndusInd Bank. The stated complaint: onboarding took 3 days with high rejection rates. But when I mapped actual drop-off data across 3 months of transaction logs, the real picture was different.

60%+ of failures happened in the first 10 minutes ‚Äî the document upload phase. Users weren't waiting for a slow process. They were failing silently and giving up. Meanwhile, each failed KYC generated a support ticket costing ~15 minutes of bank ops time.

The right metric wasn't processing speed. It was first-attempt success rate.

Discovery Process

  • Pulled 3 months of KYC transaction logs ‚Äî mapped stage-by-stage failure rates
  • Interviewed bank ops teams at Yes Bank and IndusInd to understand support ticket volume
  • Found 3 failure points: low-quality document uploads, liveness model failing in bright ambient light (common in tier-2 cities), and zero real-time feedback to users who failed

The Key Decision

The data science team pushed to retrain the AI model first ‚Äî it was the exciting problem. I pushed back. Retraining needed 8 weeks plus RBI-compliant validation. Meanwhile, 60% of failures were on document upload ‚Äî a UX problem, not an AI problem.

We shipped guided capture UI + real-time feedback first (3 weeks, zero compliance risk). First-attempt success rate improved 28% in month one. That gave us the data to justify the model retrain in Phase 2.

  • Onboarding: 3 days ‚Üí under 2 hours
  • KYC accuracy up 35%
  • Customer acquisition 40% faster
  • Platform revenue grew 25%
  • 3 major contract renewals secured

Key learning: In B2B, your user's user is often the one you need to understand. The banks were my clients ‚Äî but the people struggling with KYC were their customers. I had to understand both to solve it.

Deloitte Digital
Product Consultant ¬∑ Nov 2021‚ÄìJan 2024
Which GenAI Feature Do You Build First? ($10M Program)

Every stakeholder wanted "AI in the contact center." Nobody could say what that meant. I mapped 180+ agent tasks, made the sequencing call, and delivered a $10M program 20% ahead of schedule.

60% efficiency gain 35% lower handle time $10M ¬∑ 20% ahead 10+ AI features

Context

TruServe is Deloitte's Salesforce-based contact center platform. In 2022, every enterprise client wanted GenAI ‚Äî but no one had a clear product brief. My job on the $10M Health Care Service Corporation (HCSC) program: figure out which AI features to build and in what order.

HCSC: ~1M customer interactions/year, 2,000+ agents, 40% of time spent on post-call documentation.

Discovery

I ran structured workshops across 3 HCSC business units and mapped 180+ agent tasks on two dimensions: automation potential and business impact. The result changed everything:

  • Smart email drafting ‚Äî high automation potential, high impact, low downside risk
  • Real-time agent guidance ‚Äî very high upside, but catastrophic if wrong during a live call
  • Post-call documentation ‚Äî high volume, high potential, medium risk

The Sequencing Bet

Leadership wanted the flagship feature: real-time AI agent assist. I argued against it for Phase 1. Wrong guidance during a live healthcare call = compliance liability. The model needed training data we didn't have yet.

I proposed smart email drafting first: saves 8‚Äì12 min/email, easy to override, easy to measure. They agreed. 78% of AI drafts were accepted without significant edits in sprint 4. That number unlocked Phase 2.

Delivery Challenges

  • Scope creep: New GPT capabilities announced monthly. Introduced formal change control ‚Äî every addition required impact assessment on timeline, budget, and integration complexity.
  • Adoption: Agents were skeptical. Added AI confidence scores to the UI so agents felt in control. Adoption improved ~30% vs. baseline assumptions.
  • Cross-org alignment: HCSC IT, Legal, Operations, and Business had different definitions of "done." Monthly joint reviews with all four ‚Äî slow but prevented a compliance-related 6-week delay.

üìä Outcome

  • 60% operational efficiency improvement at HCSC's contact center
  • Agent handle time reduced 35%
  • $10M program delivered 20% ahead of schedule
  • 10+ GenAI features shipped including email drafting, post-call docs, QA sentiment flagging
  • Became an internal Deloitte case study for GenAI transformation methodology

Key learning: "AI" is not a product requirement. A job-to-be-done is. In regulated industries, the cost of being wrong is asymmetric ‚Äî a 90%-right AI that's 10%-wrong in dangerous ways is worse than doing nothing. This shaped every prioritization call I made.

Deciding What Not to Build: Roadmap Strategy at Enterprise Scale

50+ features, 6 release slots, multiple tier-1 insurance clients all calling their request the highest priority. The real challenge wasn't execution ‚Äî it was clarity.

‚Üì 30% onboarding time ‚Üì 25% abandonment ‚Üë 15% adoption 95% pre-prod quality

Context

Sapiens CoreSuite is an enterprise insurance platform serving 500K+ end customers through tier-1 insurance carriers globally. When I joined as Senior PO, the backlog had 50+ features with no consistent prioritization methodology. Multiple clients, competing priorities, 6 release slots.

Building the Framework

My first 30 days: understand the backlog, not execute it. I ran client interviews with 3 tier-1 clients, an architecture review to map technical dependencies, and analyzed support tickets (what clients complained about vs. what they requested ‚Äî often different).

I built a 4-factor prioritization matrix:

  • Client breadth ‚Äî how many clients benefit?
  • Strategic value ‚Äî does this advance platform differentiation or just maintain parity?
  • Technical risk ‚Äî does this depend on a complex architecture change that might slip?
  • Revenue linkage ‚Äî is this tied to a contract commitment or renewal?

The Hardest Call

Release 3: Feature A (digital self-service portal, 2 clients waiting, visible to customers) vs. Feature B (batch processing optimization, 1 client, critical for renewal). The metric said A. Revenue risk said B.

I took both to the steering committee with explicit trade-off framing. We chose B, protected the renewal, and I personally committed to the two waiting clients that Feature A would land in Release 4 with dedicated capacity ‚Äî which required de-prioritizing two smaller features and direct client communication.

üìä Outcome (6 months, ongoing)

  • Customer onboarding time reduced 30%
  • Transaction abandonment down 25%
  • Platform adoption up 15%
  • 95% pre-production quality rate ‚Äî zero P1 defects shipped to clients
  • 50+ features triaged with full stakeholder alignment across 6 releases

Key learning: Enterprise clients don't just want features ‚Äî they want confidence that the platform is moving in the right direction. Roadmap predictability is itself a product. Every clean release is a trust deposit.

Iksula Services
Business Analyst ‚Üí PM ¬∑ Jul 2019‚ÄìOct 2021
Learning Product the Hard Way: 6 Products, 3 Brands, 2 Years

Every client said "our checkout is broken." Checkout was fine ‚Äî discovery was broken. I made the counter-intuitive call, shipped a personalization engine, and moved conversion 16 points.

+16pp conversion 6 products shipped 95% client sat Excellence Award

Context

Iksula helped premium Indian brands ‚Äî ITC, Eureka Forbes, ELC Toys ‚Äî build their own e-commerce channels as Amazon ate into their direct sales. My role: ship products end-to-end, from discovery to go-to-market, on multiple client accounts simultaneously.

The Diagnosis Everyone Got Wrong

Industry default answer to low conversion: optimize checkout. We tried it. The needle barely moved. When I mapped the full user journey via session recordings and behavioral analysis, the real picture emerged:

  • Returning users saw the same homepage as new users ‚Äî zero personalization
  • A customer who bought baby toys last month was seeing ITC sauce promotions
  • Mobile abandonment was 60%+ ‚Äî but it happened at the product detail page, not payment

The problem was discovery, not checkout. Users left because they couldn't find what they wanted.

The Bet

The personalization engine was riskier than checkout optimization: more expensive, harder to measure, invisible to clients in a demo. I made the case to ITC's digital team with a competitive benchmark: Amazon's product pages for the same items vs. ITC's own site. The difference was personalization. They approved it.

I ran A/B tests for 8 weeks to reach statistical significance. Results:

  • Personalized homepage ‚Üí +8pp conversion vs. control
  • Mobile PDP restructure ‚Üí +6pp on mobile
  • Abandoned cart emails (personalized) ‚Üí 22% recovery rate

üìä Outcome

  • Conversion rates improved +16 percentage points across accounts
  • 6 products shipped end-to-end, on time
  • 95% client satisfaction score across all accounts
  • Quarterly Excellence Award ‚Äî first given for "product ownership mindset" in a BA role

Key learning: The presenting problem is rarely the actual problem. Every client said checkout ‚Äî but checkout was fine. I learned to be deeply skeptical of the first diagnosis and to look at the full journey before picking a solution. This habit has stayed with me ever since.

I start with the user, not the feature

Before writing a user story, I want to understand who it's for, what they're trying to do, and what's in their way. Discovery first ‚Äî always.

I make trade-offs visible

Prioritization shouldn't be a black box. I use frameworks (RICE, value/effort, MoSCoW) and make every trade-off explicit to stakeholders before committing to a roadmap.

I treat engineering as a partner

The best decisions I've made came from conversations with engineers who said "there's a smarter way." Technical feasibility is a product constraint, not a blocker.

I don't confuse output with outcome

Shipped features are inputs. What matters is whether they moved the metric that mattered. I track post-launch performance obsessively ‚Äî and I'm comfortable killing features that didn't work.

I write specs people want to read

Clear acceptance criteria, explicit edge cases, annotated wireframes. A vague spec is a tax on engineering velocity.
